Spatial Analysis of Working-Age Population in North Carolina

Local Moran’s I (LISA) clustering of percent working-age (18–64) by census tract — Nguyen_GEOG215_ProjectCheckIn_1 — Khoi Nguyen (2025-05-29)

Abstract

This project maps and analyzes the geographic distribution of the working-age population (18–64) across North Carolina census tracts using ACS estimates and Local Indicators of Spatial Association (LISA). By building spatial weights, computing Local Moran’s I, and classifying cluster types (High–High, Low–Low, High–Low, Low–High), the analysis identifies workforce-rich metropolitan areas and regions dominated by older or seasonal populations. Results highlight urban concentration of working-age adults in Raleigh–Durham and Charlotte, and Low–Low clusters along coastal and mountain counties. Findings inform workforce planning and spatial equity discussions.

Data & Methods

Data sources: American Community Survey (ACS 5-year estimates 2018–2022), TIGER/Line tract geometries for NC.
Software: R (RStudio), packages: tidycensus, tigris, sf, spdep, rgeoda, and tmap.

Key processing steps

  1. Downloaded ACS sex/age variables for working-age groups (male and female 18–64) at tract level and summed them into a single working-age population variable.
  2. Retrieved total population and calculated percent working-age = 100 × working_age_pop / total_pop.
  3. Joined attribute data to NC tract geometries and cleaned large fields (removed ALAND/AWATER before shapefile export).
  4. Built spatial weights using queen contiguity and computed Global Moran’s I to confirm significant spatial autocorrelation.
  5. Computed Local Moran’s I (LISA), derived significance measures, and classified tracts into cluster types (HH, LL, HL, LH, Not significant).
  6. Mapped results with tmap and produced figures for interpretation.

Results

  • Global spatial autocorrelation: Global Moran’s I indicated significant clustering of percent working-age (Moran I ≈ 0.28, p < 0.001).
  • High–High clusters: Concentrated in metropolitan centers (Mecklenburg/Charlotte, Wake/Raleigh, Durham).
  • Low–Low clusters: Appeared in coastal retirement regions and several mountainous/rural counties.
  • Outliers (HL/LH): Identified localized tracts such as small urban pockets in otherwise low-density counties or low- working-age tracts embedded inside high-working-age surroundings (e.g., university or tourist zones).
  • Descriptive stats: mean percent working-age ≈ 61.4%, SD ≈ 8.6% (N ≈ 2649 tracts; minor number of NAs handled).

Interpretation & Implications

The LISA cluster map confirms that economic opportunity and urbanization explain much of the working-age population distribution. High–High tracts cluster around job-rich urban corridors while Low–Low tracts correspond to retirement and low-employment regions (coastal tourism, mountainous areas). Outlier tracts highlight places of rapid local change (growing towns, campuses, military housing).

Policy implications: Identifying working-age deserts helps target workforce development, transit investment, and economic revitalization programs. Conversely, workforce concentrations suggest where housing, transit, and services need scaling.

Limitations & Future Work

  • ACS estimates have margins of error at tract scale; small-sample variation can affect classification.
  • The analysis uses point-in-time ACS data; temporal trends (migration, seasonal shifts) are not explicitly modeled.
  • Future work: incorporate employment location data, commuting flows, or network-based accessibility measures to link where people live with where jobs are located.

Reproducibility & Code

All data retrieval, processing, and map exports were performed in R with documented scripts. Key steps included: tidycensus calls to pull ACS variables, sf-based joins and cleaning, poly2nb/nb2listw for spatial weights, localmoran_perm for LISA, and tmap for cartography. Code and maps can be shared upon request.
